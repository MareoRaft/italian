Subjunctive Tense (Il Congiuntivo) top

This tense is used for things unreal or uncertain, or that we have feelings about: hopes, fears, etc.

Typically the first part of the sentence (which indicates the unreal/uncertainness) is regular, and then the following part is in the subjunctive. But the second part needs to have a different subject for it to be triggered. See fluentu for more.

Like a regular verb, remove the are/ere/iri. Add the following endings: for ARE:

i iamo
i iate
i ino
for IRE and ERE:

a iamo
a iate
a ano
Because of the ambiguity, use 'io', 'tu', and 'lui/lei' before the verb.

Example: Che peccato che lei sia lontana da me! The Use of Ne top

The word 'ne' in Italian indicates the phrase 'of it' or 'of them.' The italian word 'di' indicates the 'of' in a sentence. Once you have located the 'di' in an italian sentence, take it and the words after it out of the sentence. Add the word 'ne' before the verb.

Example: Ho paura dei topi ... I am afraid of mice
               Ne ho paura ... I am afraid of them


When using 'ne' in the past tense, you must agree the end of the past tense verb with whatever the it or them is.

Example: Quanti fragoli hai mangiato? ... How many strawberries did you eat?
               Ne ho mangiati dieci ... I ate ten of them


Please note that what normally be 'mangiato' now ends in 'i' because fragoli is plural and that is what the 'ne' is refering to.


Double Object Pronouns top

The word direct object refers to the noun after the action verb that the verb is talking about. In "I draw a picture," "picture" is the direct object. An indirect object is the noun that the direct object refers to. In the sentence, "I wrote Sara a letter," "Sara" is the indirect object because the letter (which is the direct object) was written to her. In the sentence, "I threw the ball to John," "John" is the indirect object because the ball is being thrown to him. In English, words like "for" and "to" usually come before an indirect object; In Italian, the words "a" or "con" signify an indirect object.

Direct Object Pronouns replace both the direct and indirect objects in a sentence. In the sentence, 'He gives the pencil to George,' 'pencil' is the direct and 'George' is the indirect. In English we use the pronouns 'it' and 'him' to simplify this sentence. 'He gives it to him.' In Italian, we put a double object pronoun before the verb. Example: Gioco le carte con Anna ... I play cards with Anna
               Gliele gioco ... I play them with her


When using an infinitive, attach the Double Object Pronoun to the end of the infinitive instead of before the verb.

Example: Voglio dare una mela a Cara ... I want to give a melon to Cara
               Voglio dargliela ... I want to give it to her


In the past tense, agree the ending of the verb with the direct object.

Example: Abbiamo mandato una lettera a Pucinella ... We sent a letter to Pucinella
               Gliel'abbiamo mandata ... We sent it to him



Commands (Imperativo) top



INFORMAL


1. In the INFORMAL, commands are only used in the tu, noi, and voi forms.
2. They are conjugated like normal present tense verbs, with the following exceptions.
3. For a tu conjugation of an ARE verb, use the lui/lei conjugation.
4. For a tu conjugation preceded by non, use the infinitive form of the verb.
5. Short ARE verbs such as stare can either become sta` or stai in the tu form.
6. Dire is an irregular. It becomes di` for all conjugations.

There are two other irregular verbs: Avere and Essere. The tu, noi, and voi forms are shown below (yes, for INFORMAL) (No, it's not EXACTLY the same as subjunctive tense).

For reflexive verbs, conjugate them normally (even for the tu form) and add the following endings. ti for tu, ci for noi, and vi for voi.

If you are using an indirect object pronoun with a short are verb in the tu form, such as fare, you attach it to the end of the conjugated verb and double the first letter of the indirect object pronoun. Fa` + mi would become Fammi for example.



FORMAL


1. Because this is FORMAL, use the Lei when talking to 'you' and the voi when talking to 'yall' (the Loro is never used in modern italian).
2. Use the present subjunctive tense.

For example, "Sir, come with me." is "Signore, venga con me."

For example, "Sirs, come with me." is "Signori, vengate con me."

Using "si" top Si impersonale e si passivante:

Si is used to speak in general. 'In America they eat hamburgers', 'You must think before you speak', and 'Here we sing songs and dance,' are all general sentences. They do not really refer to specific people. Si is the italian equivalant. To say a general sentence in Italian, place si before the verb and put the verb in the lui/lei or loro form. If the Direct Object of the sentence is singular, you use the lui/lei form; if it is plural, use the loro form.

Example: Si studia Dante in casa ... Dante is studied in our house
               Si studiano le opere ... Operas are studied


There are three irregular phrases using si in Italian.
si sa che... it is common knowledge that... (one knows that...)
si capisce che... it is obvious that... (one understands that...)
si vede che... it is clear that... (one sees that...) If there is no object it is a si impersonale, if there is an object it's a si passivante. si impersonale --- si impersonale is always 3rd person singular. Si mangia. Si beve. Si è. But if there is an ADJECTIVE, it is PLURAL. Si è timidi. Si è strani. Nella gravidanza, si è più robuste. (note here that the adjective is feminine because the general subject is only talking about females). And in p.p. tense... Si è mangiato. (passato prossimo for si impersonale will always use essere. mangiare would normally use avere, so we end with an "o") Si è bevuto. Si è stati. (essere would normally use essere, so we end with an "i") Si è stati timidi. si passivante --- In si passivante, what looks like the object is actually the subject. Si mangia la pasta. (here we use 3rd person singular because "pasta" is the subject) Si mangiano gli spaghetti. (here we use 3rd person plural because "spaghetti" is the subject) (both "gli spaghetti si mangiano" and "gli spaghetti sono mangiati" are equivalent sentences) And in the p.p. tense... Si è mangiata la pasta. (note that the conjugation è mangiata comes naturally from the passive p.p. conjugation. La pasta è mangiata) Si sono mangiati gli spaghetti. (= gli spagetti sono mangiati, but in past tense.)


Possession top

The possessive words equivalent to 'my', 'your', etc are mio, tuo, suo, nostro, vostro, loro. You can view them here.

These by default include an article. For example, 'il mio', 'il tuo', 'il loro', etc.

If you have a OBJECT VERB POSSESSIVE pattern, the article is ommitted. For example, "that woman is mine" is "Quella donna è mia". For example, "The forest is theirs" is "La forresta è loro".

If the possessed noun is a FAMILY RELATION and in the SINGULAR and the possessor is NOT "LORO", then the article is ommitted. For example, "my sister" is "mia sorella". For example, "yalls gramma" is "vostra nona". {% endblock %}
