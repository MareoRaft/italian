Avere and Essere top

The verbs avere(to have) and essere(to be) are commonly used in Italian. They are conjugated as so:

Avere ho hai ha abbiamo avete hanno
Essere sono sei e` siamo siete sono

Sentences such as "I have a tennis racket." and "He owns the house." use avere. "Sentences such as "They are content." and "It is a dog." use essere. There are seven exceptions to this. When using these exceptions, you must use avere instead of essere. The exceptions are: avere fame (to be hungry), avere sete (to be thirsty), avere freddo (to be cold), avere caldo (to be hot), avere ragione (to be right), avere sbagliato (to be wrong), avere paura di (to be afraid of), and avere infretta (to be in a hurry). The past tense of avere is avuto and the past tense of essere is stato.

Avere and Essere are most important because they are used to make sentences past tense. To make a present tense sentence past tense, put the conjugated form of essere or avere in front of the verb and make the verb past tense. If you use essere, the ending vowel of the conjugated verbs must agree with the subject: o (singular masculine), a (singular feminine), i (plural masculine), or e (plural feminine). In order to determine whether to use essere or avere, simply ask yourself this question: "Is this something I can do going in or out the door?" If it is, use essere; if it isn't, use avere.



Conjugating Regular Verbs top

Regular verbs end in "are, "ire", or "ere" and change depending on the person using the verb. When conjugating a regular verb, take out the "are, "ire", or "ere" so that you are left with the root. Then plug in the correct verb endings. Below is a table consisting of the correct verb endings.

ARE IRE ERE
o o o
i i i
a e e
iamoiamoiamo
ate ite ete
ano ono ono

To conjugate a verb in past tense, first put the correct form of essere or avere in front of it conjugated in present tense. Essere is used for any coming or goin actions (anything you can do going through a door). Avere is used for everything else. After choosing the right form of avere or essere, you must reduce the verb to its root and add the appropriate past tense ending depending on what you took away:

ARE IRE ERE
ato uto ito

Contracting Prepositions top

When a preposition comes directly before an article, it usually contracts with the article. Here's a quick reference for you...

a con da di in su
lo allo collo dallo dello nello sullo
il al col dal del nel sul
l' all' coll' dall' dell' nell' sull'
la alla colla dalla della nella sulla
i ai coi dai dei nei sui
gli agli cogli dagli degli negli sugli
le alle colle dalle delle nelle sulle


All contractions follow the same pattern; except con becomes co before contracting, di becomes de before contracting, and in becomes ne before contracting. The preposition per(for) does not contract and the preposition con is not required to contract in the italian language and most often is left uncontracted.

*note: articles are not used with the following words: casa + scuola

*note: articles are not used after in with the following words: biblioteca, banca, campagna, centro, chiesa, piazza, rooms in a house, ufficio.



Direct Object Pronouns top

The word direct object refers to the noun after the action verb that the verb is talking about. In "I draw a picture," "picture" is the direct object. A direct object pronoun replaces the direct object with a pronoun. The sentence would now be "I draw it." Direct Object Pronouns can also refer to people.

In Italian, DOPs have these special articles:

Direct Object Pronouns
mici
tivi
lo/lali/le

The DO that you are changing to "it" will become the article it corrosponds to. Non-living objects can only use the lo/la and li/le articles. Once you have found the right article, you must move it in front of the verb. Example...

The sentence "I clean the room."
In Italian "Pulisco la stanza."
Change "room" to "it" "Pulisco la."
Place "it" before the verb "La pulisco."

The sentence "We saw Cara and Frank."
In Italian "Abbiamo veduto Cara e Franko."
Change "Cara and Frank" to "them" "Abbiamo veduto li."
Place "them" before the verb "Li abbiamo veduto."

Notice that "li" went before "abbiamo" and not just "veduto." This is because avere and essere are verbs also and they help out the second verb. They must always stick to the verb. Do not make the mistake of writing "Abbiamo li veduto" on your tests!



Indirect Object Pronouns top

Indirect Object Pronouns are extreemly similar to direct object pronouns. An indirect object is the noun that the direct object refers to. In the sentence, "I wrote Sara a letter," "Sara" is the indirect object because the letter (which is the direct object) was written to her. In the sentence, "I threw the ball to John," "John" is the indirect object because the ball is being thrown to him. In English, words like "for" and "to" usually come before an indirect object; In Italian, the words "a" or "con" signify an indirect object. Using indirect objects in italian involves the same process as that for direct objects, but the articles are different.

Indirect Object Pronouns
mici
tivi
gli/legli/loro


The sentence "I call to Alison."
In Italian "Chiamo a lei."
Change "Alison" to "her" "Chiamo a le."
Place "her" before the verb "Le chiamo."
